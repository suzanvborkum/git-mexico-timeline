A HISTORY OF

MEXICO

MIGUEL HIDALGO CRIES
THE GRITO DE DOLORES

Miguel Hidalgo cries the Grito de Dolores

On September 16 1810 priest Miguel Hidalgo speaks to a crowd in Dolores Hidalgo and says the famous words:

¡viva la Virgen de Guadalupe!, ¡viva Fernando VII!, ¡viva la América y muera el mal gobierno!

These words marked the beginning of the independence.

THE BATTLE OF MONTE DE LAS CRUCES

The battle of Monte de Las Cruces

A month and a half after Hidalgo called for a war of independence, the rebels moved to Mexico-City. Hidalgo and his insurgent movement succeeded in overtaking a handful cities around Guanajuato. On October 30, 1810 the rebels reached Monte de las Cruces, 50 kilometers west of Mexico-City. Surprisingly the poorly organized army of Hildalgo beat the royalists. Even though the road to Mexico-City was now free, Hidalgo made the decision to retreat.

1810

EXECUTION OF MIGUEL HIDALGO

Execution of Miguel Hidalgo

The 1st of January of 1811 was not a good day for the insurgents. In the battle of the bridge of Calderon, the army was defeated by the Spanish and Miguel Hidalgo and his fellow rebel leaders were captured. On July 30, 1811 Hidalgo is stripped of his priesthood, found guilty, and executed. Hidalgo's body was mutilated and his head was displayed in Guanajuato as a warning to the Mexican rebels.

1811

PLAN OF IGUALA PROMULGATED

Plan of Iguala

Agustin de Iturbide was a royalist who, in fear of losing his power, wanted to end the independence movement. Iturbide created a three-part proposal, the Plan of Iguala, summarized as Religión, Independencia y Unión. On 24 February 1821 Iturbide and Vincente Guerrero promulgated the proposal. Mexico would gain independence, but be a monarchy. Second, creoles and peninsulares would have equal rights. Last, Roman Catholic Church would be the official church of Mexico.

MEXICAN INDEPENDENCE
IS A FACT

Mexican independence is a fact

With signing the Treaty of Cordoba on August 24, 1821 the Mexican war of independence comes to an end and the independence is a fact (even though Spain did not recognize Mexico's independence until December 28, 1836). Iturbide was proclaimed emperor of Mexico on May 18, 1822, a monarchy that lasted only three years.

1821

MEXICO LOSES TEXAS IN THE BATTLE OF SAN JACINTO

Battle of San Jacinto

On October 2, 1835 rebels put up an armed resistance against the central government of Mexico for an independent Texas. Since Mexico’s independence the government allowed immigrants from the United States into the region. The new protestant inhabitants did not convert to the Mexican Catholicism and friction started to immerge. The Texas Revolution came to an end during the Battle of San Jacinto on April 21, 1836 which announced the independence of Texas.

1836

MEXICAN-AMERICAN WAR BEGINS

Mexican-American war begins

From 1833 until 1853 Antonio López de Santa Anna was a household name in Mexico. He was president off and on also during the invasion of the Americans in Mexico. After rejections to surrender from the Mexicans, the American army worked its way south from Monterrey to Vera Cruz, until they reached Mexico City in the fall of 1847. Los Niños Héroes (Boy Heroes) were summoned by officers to defend Chapultepec until their death. The myth that the last boy jumped of the roof of the castle wrapped into the Mexican flag is still a much-told story. Unfortunately it was of no avail and Mexico broke its defense.

1846

MEXICO LOSES TERRITORY TO THE UNITED STATES

Mexico loses Territory to the Unites States

On February 2, 1848 the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo is signed, deciding that the United States needs to pay 15 millions USD to Mexico, but Mexico is forced to sell modern day California, Nevada, New Mexico, Arizona and Utah. The disputes around the border stayed for many decades.

1848

BENITO JUAREZ BECOMES PRESIDENT OF MEXICO

Benito Juarez becomes president of Mexico

In every town in Mexico you find a statue of Benito Juarez, the Zapotec reformer, democrat and Mexico’s most beloved president. His presidency was colored by the Guerra de Reforma and the French intervention, but until this day his legacy is mostly known for introducing the 'Juarez Law', which declared all citizens equal before the law and restricted the privileges of the Catholic Church and the Mexican army. He remained president until 1872, when he died of a heart attach in his office.
